    • DrumoCat

      Applications

      The DrumoCat program is an interactive application developed in Processing that connects to a MIDI e-drum kit. At its core, the program features a cat character situated behind a drum kit, whose arms move to strike the corresponding drum parts on-screen with preloaded sound files (e.g., snare.wav, kick.wav) providing the audio.

      Key features include recording drum inputs, saving sequences, and applying transformations like quantization (snapping beats to a tempo) and BPM adjustments (modifying timestamps rather than playback speed). Additional tools include an MP3 player, BPM tapper, metronome, intro trimming, and drumbeat visualizers.

      Initially, implementing quantization seemed impossible when working with .wav or .mp3 files, as there was no direct access to manipulate individual drum sounds or timings. This limitation forced me to reevaluate my approach. Instead of recording system sound, I designed a system to record MIDI-like data by capturing the timestamp (in milliseconds), pitch (drum part), and velocity (strike intensity). I created a MidiEvent class to store this data, along with an ArrayList called recordedEvents to log the input and another sequenceFiles list to manage loaded drum beats for manipulation. With the drumbeat stored in memory, quantization became feasible. To quantize, I calculated the duration of a quarter note in milliseconds based on the BPM using: 1 (ms) = 60,000 / BPM. For example, at 120 BPM, 1 beat = 500ms (4/4 time signature only). Quantizing snapped each event's timestamp to the nearest beat division.

    • Honours Thesis

      AI

      Title: Exploring Transfer Learning in Deep Reinforcement Learning for Fighting Games

      My honours project takes on a journey through how I trained AI to master one game and then use that knowledge to dominate another. The two main games we are focusing on is Street Fighter II and Fatal Fury 2.

      Methodology: To test transfer learning, I trained three models across two games—Street Fighter II and Fatal Fury 2—and used additional untrained models for comparison. A custom environment was developed, featuring preprocessing techniques like grayscaling, frame resizing, and stacking the last four frames. This gave the AI temporal context, allowing it to ‘see’ how the environment changes over time and react strategically.

      Results: In Street Fighter II, the baseline model (M1) achieved a high win rate of 83%, compared to an empty model’s 52%. When tested on Fatal Fury 2 without fine-tuning, the transferred model still outperformed the empty model on Fatal Fury 2 which averaged a 7%, where the Street Figher II model achieved a 34% win rate. Fine-tuning M1 by training the model on Fatal Fury (M3) improved this to 36%, but training from scratch on Fatal Fury (M2) yielded a slightly higher win rate of 39%. This demonstrates that although transfer learning without fine tuning yields strong performance when compared to the likes of an empty model, fine-tuning is essential for domain adaptation. But even then the model from scratch still outperformed the fine tuned model.

      However, there is an interesting observation. When training the model on Fatal Fury 2 with weights transferred from Street Fighter II (M3) compared to training a model from scratch on Fatal Fury 2 (M2),the model with transferred weights (M3) significantly outperformed the model trained from scratch until eventually they both broke even at around 2.644M timestep as seen in this graph, where the M3 is the red line and M2 is the blue line. A significant claim can be made highlighting that transfer learning does indeed work and does save a significant amount of time, as compared to just training the model from scratch (M2). I then tested M3 on Street Fighter II again to see if it retained the peformance it was initially trained on and surprising it did with a relative high win rate of 77%, proving transfer learning's generalisation.

    • Sentiment Analysis for Movie Reviews

      AI

      Title: Using Sentiment Analysis to Compare Critics and Audience Movie Reviews

      This research project was conducted in collaboration with five other academic peers under the supervision of a postgraduate researcher and was part of my Computing Science Studio 2, a subject designed to simulate real industry research experience.

      The project explores the differences between online movie reviews provided by critics and audiences through sentiment analysis. By leveraging the pretrained RoBERTa model from Hugging Face, we analyzed large datasets of movie reviews, classifying sentiments as positive, neutral, or negative.

      The research aimed to uncover key distinctions in how critics and audiences evaluate movies. While critics often focus on cinematic elements such as storytelling, direction, and underlying themes, audiences primarily base their reviews on entertainment value and emotional resonance. Our findings revealed that critics tend to be more analytical and provide a wider range of scores, whereas audiences generally rate movies based on enjoyment, leading to a narrower score range.

      The project also highlights how nostalgia plays a significant role in audience reviews, especially for genres like real-life drama and children's movies. On the other hand, genres perceived as formulaic, such as comedy and horror, tend to receive lower scores from critics. Understanding these differences provides valuable insights for filmmakers, critics, and audiences alike.
